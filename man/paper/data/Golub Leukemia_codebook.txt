The Golub Leukemia dataset, often cited in Trevor Hastie’s work (such as Computer Age Statistical Inference or The Elements of Statistical Learning), is a landmark gene expression dataset from a 1999 Science paper by Golub et al. 

It is commonly used for classification tasks distinguishing between two types of acute leukemia: Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) and Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML).

Here are the key details regarding the dataset:
Dataset OverviewSource: Golub et al. (1999), "Molecular Classification of Cancer: Class Discovery and Class Prediction by Gene Expression Monitoring".

Purpose: Classifying tumor samples into AML or ALL based on gene expression levels.Structure: The dataset generally consists of 72 samples (patients) from bone marrow or peripheral blood.
Features: It contains 7,129 gene expression levels, measured using Affymetrix high-density oligonucleotide microarrays.

Commonly Used VersionsFull Dataset (often used in textbooks): 
Includes 7,129 genes and 72 samples.
Smaller Subset (CASIdata): A common subset used in Hastie's Computer Age Statistical Inference (Section 19.1) 
includes 3,571 genes and 72 samples.
Training/Test Split: The original study divided the data into a training set (38 samples) and an independent test set (34 samples).

Key CharacteristicsClasses: 
47 cases of Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) and 25 cases of Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML).
Dimensions: It is a high-dimensional dataset (\(p \gg n\)), meaning there are far more features (genes) than observations (patients), 
making it ideal for feature selection and classification techniques like 
SVM or logistic regression.
Normalization: The data is usually provided normalized, with expression values scaled to allow comparison across microarrays.
